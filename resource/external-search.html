This article is called external-search as I am often employed to create or extend search with a company's dataset. External-search is concentrating on publicly accessible data (including things that content creators forgot was public, like word files), and rarely needs identification or authentication.
In 2003 I wrote my first article on search and comparative features. Every few years I write a new one, with different content. NOTE: search as an industry seems to be sensitive to people checking if links have been changed. My tooltip feature is just so I don't sent you to dead links, if the tooltip says dead the remote website may have lied to the link checker.

Note: market success percentages for each engine should be taken as an approximation. If you go to three different news sites you get three different values. I think the market percentage ratios between the engines won't change.

Note: I list the carbon neutral scoring, as running an LLM on a widely used service is a very heavy consumer for drinking water and power. LLM directly use a lot of power, and the drinking water is used for the air-con in the server farm. Server farms do alter the water table in arid regions; so other all local businesses are affected. Especially when linked to the fact that the larger corporations rarely pay taxes, and push local housing prices up, this has a strong impact on the local economy. I expect users may be casually interested in-passing, but please do not take this as a heavy-handed hint about anything.

UPDATE: If for some reason you need to use an AI, I have been recommended about: Perplexity and Poe/Claude

Important features for search

  • takes search query, returns 3rd party site URLs that seem to be about the query
  • supports typo fixing AND
  • supports booleans, literal strings and +/- terms
    • literal strings suppress typo fixing
    • +/- terms allow a query to be in a particular domain, not the most common one
  • supports site constraint
  • supports age limit constraints
  • allow deletion of any profile data harvested about the user, or the users unique ids
  • everything that is a paid-ad is marked as “paid ad” in some fashion ~ when searching for “wet weather saab tires”, ads are useful to the user, when searching for “model hydration error saab catalogue”, ads for car parts are less so.

Recent history

I am not impressed with the current generation of LLM that has been blindly applied to many websites. For example the AI that Linkedin uses has said I ~ the candidate ~ shouldn't apply for a particular role as I ~ the candidate ~ didn't have “Employee Benefits”. We can read data from /dev/random at much lower effort and expense. In theory LLM are useful 1; but they need cleaner data first. The US free speech rules mean that many US people post stuff that is outside of 'corporate voice'. This shouldn't be included in the LLM dataset.

The UK market for search

This article now focusses on the UK search engine. If mr Mishra is correct, and most search is local; then it makes sense to have a local engine, targeting local websites. I don't think a resident of Tokyo is interested in the opening hours of my local Sainsburies, also I get little value from knowing the opening hours of their Seiyu. We can ignore each other for this item by spelling-out the name of the premise, in the normal language used to communicate. Noting I normally type “supermarket near me open now”, there are 8 large ones inside 5km, I assume the same would be true for of most of Tokyo.

As a description of Search as market, the UK is about 70 million people, 92% of which are fairly often internet users 1 ~ this is 2019 numbers, note Covid has changed everything. The lowest net-use ~ not surprisingly ~ is the elderly, but net-use is now at 54% in that segment. Uswitch says most people have a smart phone, and about half have a big screen device on the internet 2. Statistica also state that 93% of people use Google, but less use Bing than above 3. And “the UK is responsible for almost four percent of Google.com’s worldwide traffic, with its own powerful local domain, Google.co.uk." 4. According to 5 the most popular query to Google this year was “google”, and the next top 10 items was where mostly other net brands. The outlier was “weather”.
Note: The ONS “people who recently used the internet” number matches Statistica “people who recently used the internet from a smart phone” which is weird.

Search summary

Google inc is a very large corporation that is very profitable, and spends vast amounts on R&D to keep their position in the market. They have strong data capture and user profiling features to be able to sell targeted advertising. After ChatGPT did a few successful demos, all the main stream search engines thought they needed to make a solution with an AI to keep current market share. In this context “move fast and break stuff” seems risky near elections.
I looked at alternatives, and the results are listed here. I think reducing the data that users feed google would be a smart idea; and I make claims about search locality, to make search relevance easier.
