Expected audience: software engineers who have clients/ bosses in the UK. This is the most “senior engineer article” on this site to-date. This article is extensive, therefore quite long.

The current Tory administration is working to improve the currency and relevancy of UK law regarding “dem interweb finngies”. A longer version of this text would reference the educational attainments of the people proposing the laws, and so a basic observation of their domain knowledge. Some UK law is well written/ wise, e.g. the law regulating the public carrying of random things that seem to indicate premeditated violence, the current state 1, but my initial statement was thinking about the 1950's edition 2. This is a factual article, I will edit until this is as objective as other texts on this site.

Note con sottovoci, an actual Tory MP, in Parliament (their place of work) claimed they used “dem interwebs finngies” so badly, whilst they were looking for notes about Tractors for an Agricultural proposal, they accidentally mis-tapped and looked at pron for a while, got called up for it; then did the same thing again 1 2. I do not look at pron at work (or otherwise), I work hard instead.

Domain jargon: work-in-progress is called a bill, a completed piece is called an act 1. The initial step / call to arms / plan is called a white paper. I believe the two later steps are published on different coloured paper. Unelected and unaccountable 'Think tanks' ~ which are not part of the government ~ also make white papers. The CPS is the Crown Prosecution Service 2, the organisation that enforces criminal law in the UK, other countries have “public prosecutors” instead. As a smaller thing than an Act, there are “UK Statutory Instruments” (SI). These have narrower activity scope and are often narrower in terms of geography. This text list Acts, rather than SI. The UKG is my abbreviation for “United Kingdom government” whilst being vague about which one. What's “pron”? It's a spoonerism, there is at least 1 really stupid content filter that fails to know that “talking about X without showing it” is not the same as “here is lots of X”, please read 3 4 ~ the second link is from DE, but same limited tech applies. NOTE: this article is about UK laws, it's perfectly child-safe and has no pictures/videos at all.

Legal theories

  • Term “privacy”: the right to keep one's personal matters and relationships secret. General content wiki, and specific UK content wiki
    • A handy aggregation link by UKG on privacy in the law 5
  • Term “data ownership”: denotes the assignment of rights and responsibilities for a data to an individual or an organisation, linked to copyright wiki
    • UKG position 6 7, few solicitor agencies' position, worth reading 8 9.
  • Term “Freedom of expression” the right of freedom to hold opinions and to receive and impart information and ideas without interference by public authority and regardless of frontiers wiki
    • UKG position, reports of session 10 11
  • List of principals for GDPR 12 ~ there are 8.

Statutes

Laws are written in legal english, to reduce time in court-cases. For casual readers, this is less useful, so I include as many wiki summaries and guidances as possible.

  • Computer Misuse Act 1990 ~ to secure computer material against unauthorised access or modification text, wiki.
    1. There has been 13 editions of this act since 1990, the most recent is 2023 13.
    2. CPS annotated edition 14.
    3. For people building systems, it's less relevant, but to be used as a prop when needed.
  • DPA or Data Protection Act - currently 2018 edition text, wiki ~ the first set of laws saying that companies must handle data securely and reliably. They must have procedures to obtain, audit and delete data, as described in the 8 rights/ principals.
    1. There are 22 editions and alterations to the DPA 15.
    2. Has a specific section barring biometric or profiling data (e.g. the skin tone of your users).
    3. Replaces DPA 1998 text ~ this edition was covered in my education.
    4. An 'explainer' that may make the real text more accessible (video or text option) 16.
    5. For developers, this should add a section to your good practice guide, but ideally this law adds nothing new to your process.
  • Investigatory Powers Act 2016, text this is summarised clearly as wiki. It details were due process can override an individuals privacy.
    1. This is known in the press as the “snoopers charter”.
    2. There are amendments in-flight at present 17.
    3. Note: this also codifies that UK MPs cannot be monitored without consent from the PM. “Secure and important data” professions like lawyers and journalists also have more protection 18, an explainer 19.
    4. If you work in a Telco, this is important legislation.
  • Online Safety Act 2023 text, wiki 20 It places specific obligations on service providers to ensure the safety of online users ~ content filtering.
    1. An extension to block some mobile Apps, enforced with realistic size fines 21.
    2. This was initially called “Online Harms Bill” 22.
    3. The UKG claim this is the first law to this effect in the world 23.
  • Digital Economy Act 2017 original text, wiki addresses policy issues related to electronic communications infrastructure and services, and updates the conditions for and sentencing of criminal copyright infringement.
    1. There was an earlier Digital Economy Act 2010 which never became law.
    2. Most recent edition 24, editions 1 act, 12 SI see 25.
    3. Official guidance 26.
  • Intellectual Property Act 2014 text, wiki is to update copyright law, in particular design and patent law.
    1. Contains 1 act and 6 SI from 2014 to 2016 27.
    2. This is a separate Act, but as described 28 29.
    3. Developers building patented systems need to follow/ know this.
  • Network and Information Systems Regulations 2018 (“NIS Regulations”) text.
    1. This started out as an EU directive 1.
    2. It regulates as a second piece of legislation, that services must have security process 2 especially when they handle customer data. A 13 page guidance 3.
    3. For developers, attention to this is important.
  • The Telecommunications (Security) Act 2021 text, wiki 4.
    1. This regulates to force Telcos to have secure practice and policy, the aim is to make net digital attacks by other nations harder to perform.
    2. An updated version 5 for 2022.
    3. For the targeted industry, this probably makes a new department.
  • Copyright, Design and Patents Act 1988 text, wiki the legal right that protects the use of your work once your idea has been physically expressed.
    1. The guidance on this runs to more than 300 pages, see wiki instead.
    2. There have been 2 editions and many instruments 6. Unlike other sections of law, the Isle of Man (a crown dependency) is a focus point, partially as the IoM is outside of some international regulations, as it's not legally part of the UK.
    3. A software engineer needs to know about the sections relevant to Copyright and Design copyrights 7. A clear UK based example of boundaries 8 for copyright (employer vs employee).
    4. A developer needs to understand basic ownership laws, and probably does.
  • Copyright (Computer Programs) Regulations of 1992 text is an extension to include software inside the definition of copyright.
    1. This extension declares no impact on work older than 1993.
  • Crown copyright wiki currently this is legacy law for things that “the crown” as a person, its government or their agents manufactures.
    1. This doesn't have an explicit statute, but try reading 9.
    2. before the 17th century, the monarch regulated printing presses by granting monopolies (see wiki).
    3. As a developer, you need to know that some things in the UK may not be copied, except via an official process. The most notable items are OS maps, and birth certificates. The second point is why no finance identity check uses a birth cert.
  • More details about laws for maintenance of IP are not covered in this, as this is quite a large topic..
  • Protection of Freedoms Act 2012 text, wiki ~ part of “Great Repeal Bill” aka “Freedom (Great Repeal) Bill”, this reduces powers with automated surveillance, and utilisation of biometric data.
    1. There are 34 SI and 1 act 10.
    2. This act also allows a person to become married in the evening, which for some reason was illegal since 1836 (??!).
    3. If a developer is in the relevant market, this is relevant. Otherwise too tangential to be relevant.
  • Freedom of Information Act 2000 text, wiki ~ This overlaps other legislation, but grants random UK people access to information held by public authorities.
    1. There is 1 UK act and 7 UK SI, BUT 14 Scot SI and 2 Scot acts 11.
    2. Implementing this has led to some incidents, where personal data was accidentally released (e.g. police officers names released in Northern Ireland).
  • Fraud Act 2006 text, wiki legislation to make asymmetric deceptive sales illegal.
    1. This has no amendments, but there are lots of Statutory instruments 12 ~ there are several acts reported on this.
    2. This is a general “anti fraud” act but it describes phishing in the notes section 2, paragraph 16.
    3. This legislation is tangential to developers, as long as their invoices are tidy.
  • Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 (“RIPA 2000”) text, wiki this regulates data capture by police and “spy agencies”.
    1. This is only 1 act, and currently 23 SI 13.
    2. This may not have any impact for software engineers. Process for investigators 14.
  • Official Secrets Acts 1989 text only relevant to some situations. Some data relevant to the UKG and MoD may not be published via any mechanism.
    1. The first version was 1889, there have been 13 new editions and amendments 15.
    2. Unless you are working for the MoD, probably has no impact to technical people.
    3. An update doc 16.
  • Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act became law in 2023 text, wiki.
    1. As this law is new, the wiki is sparse, try 17 18. The Law Society says wait until the guidance is published for how to implement this.
    2. For tech staff who are not directors, no impact. For tech people who are directors, certain types of fraud and money laundering are now more difficult (says the law). Note, it claims that SLAPPS should become more rare 19.
  • An upcoming bill Data Protection and Digital Information Bill text, 20 21 22.
  • An upcoming “Criminal Justice Bill”, is a revision, that amongst other details adds in law ability to remove publicly accessible web infra when it has been used for serious crime 23 24. With international scope. Obviously details on this item may change, as it goes through parliament.
  • As an article that is not currently a law, but may become a proposal in future. Guidance for AI 25 26. Note: this list got appended to before I could get the complete draft published, this is why some sort of resource is needed.

This article is very useful and quite clear for the legal profession 27. A similar one by the civil service 28.

To show a new website isn't pron

I am adding this section, as I previously worked for a new startup, who, when they made their first edition sales funnel which was quite bland, was blocked. The first version of the website text was made by a UK barrister, as this startup was his sidejob. Barristers are obviously precise with wording. My ISP which is on default settings, described it as pron. As far as I know that domain had never previously be registered by other organisations. So ISPs “intelligent blocking”, is actually “new domain blocking”, which is easier, but not very smart.

The activity list for a new company site is:

  • Buy and setup domain,
  • Make it not blocked by ISPs,
  • get content added, also see seo
  • get HTTPS established
  • add SEO DNS headers, HTTP headers, HTML headers, assets, etc
  • added to search engines
  • spampromote presence in SM
  • if needed, add PPC adverts

To be able to see if your website is currently being blocked, view the open rights group checker. In the UK, being blocked here, will limit 3.5 million households and many mobile connections from being able to view your legitimate business.
